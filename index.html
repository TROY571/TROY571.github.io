Drug Offense in San Francisco stays concentrated even when I extend the story to the full 2003-2025 record

This version uses the full window from January 1, 2003 to December 31, 2025. Following the mapping in Assignment 1, I harmonize DRUG/NARCOTIC, Drug Offense, and Drug Violation into one category. Across that longer history, the same pattern survives: the category stays concentrated in Tenderloin, breaks sharply during the COVID years, and follows a daytime rhythm that closely tracks Warrant.

Dataset and framing

The full dataset is the combined San Francisco Police Department incident file we worked with in Weeks 1-7. For this story I use the subset from January 1, 2003 to December 31, 2025. Each row is one recorded police incident, and that matters here, because a recorded Drug Offense is not the same thing as all drug use in a neighborhood. It depends on public visibility, police presence, and enforcement choices.

To keep the category consistent across the whole period, I use the same harmonization rule as in Assignment 1: Drug Offense combines DRUG/NARCOTIC, Drug Offense, and Drug Violation. I do the same kind of cleanup for district names and for Warrant, so the long-run figures are actually comparable.

Question

If Drug Offense is shaped by enforcement as well as underlying activity, what should the data look like? I would expect it to be selective: concentrated in a few places, concentrated at certain hours, and unstable when street conditions change.

1. The long-run view still points to concentration, not a smooth citywide trend

Once I extend the series back to 2003, the story actually gets stronger. The harmonized category is high in the mid-2000s, declines through the 2010s, breaks again during 2020-2021, and rebounds by 2025. That is not the shape of a stable citywide background rate. It is a category that moves with history, policing conditions, and the visibility of street activity.

The district panel makes the point spatially. Even after using the full 2003-2025 window, Tenderloin's Drug Offense share is still far above the citywide baseline. Mission and Southern are elevated too, but they are nowhere near the same scale. So the concentration is not just a short COVID-era artifact. It is built into the longer record.

2. The daily pattern looks more like police contact than nightlife

The second figure keeps the same harmonized category but switches from long-run trend to daily rhythm. The heatmap peaks from late morning into the afternoon across most weekdays. That is not what I would expect if Drug Offense were mainly a nightlife category. It looks much closer to a daytime street-enforcement rhythm.

The lower panel makes the point sharper by comparing Drug Offense with Warrant, also harmonized across old and new labels. In my Assignment 1 analysis, this pair had the strongest weekly relationship (R2 = 0.927). Their hourly curves almost overlap here as well. My reading is that these two categories often come out of the same police encounter: someone is stopped, drugs are found, and warrant status is checked at the same time.

3. At street scale, the map collapses into a Tenderloin-centered cluster

The map is now a year-by-year animation, which makes the spatial story easier to trust. Instead of looking at one pooled map, I can watch the hotspot move through time. What stands out is not dramatic relocation. The heat stays centered around the Tenderloin and nearby downtown blocks for most of the record, even though the overall intensity changes from year to year.

The COVID years fit into that same picture. The animation weakens during 2020-2021, but the hotspot does not suddenly jump to another part of San Francisco. Outside reporting helps here: city reporting on open-air drug market enforcement repeatedly points to Tenderloin and South of Market as focus areas. That does not prove a single causal story, but it does make the animated hotspot pattern easier to interpret.

Takeaway

What makes this category interesting is that the full-range version tells the same story as the short-range version. Drug Offense is concentrated in one district, concentrated in a small set of street cells, and concentrated in a daytime schedule that mirrors Warrant.

So my conclusion is still not "Tenderloin has X times more drug use." That would claim more than police incident data can support. A narrower conclusion fits the data much better: in this harmonized 2003-2025 record, Drug Offense behaves like a public, enforcement-shaped category. It tells us where drug activity becomes visible enough to be recorded, not where all drug use exists.
