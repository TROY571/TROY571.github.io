Short Data Story

Crime in San Francisco is concentrated, not random

The combined SFPD incident dataset contains more than three million recorded incidents from 2003 onward. Read as a whole, it does not show crime spreading evenly across the city or across the day. It shows concentration: in a few years, in a few hours, and in a few places. This story follows that pattern from annual totals to daily rhythm to geographic hotspot.

Dataset and framing

The data comes from San Francisco Police Department incident reports that were cleaned and combined during the course. Each row is a recorded incident, not a direct measure of harm, victimization, or resident behavior. That distinction matters: changes in policing, reporting practices, and record systems can move the counts even when underlying behavior changes less.

With that caveat in place, the dataset is still useful for asking where incidents accumulate and when they tend to happen. Those are the questions this page focuses on.

Story claim

The strongest signal in this dataset is concentration. Recorded incidents cluster into specific years, specific hours, and specific central-city locations. That makes a general citywide average less informative than a targeted view of time and place.

1. Annual totals move, but not in a straight line

A first look at the full series shows why a single headline like "crime went up" or "crime went down" is too simple. The counts are relatively high in the mid-2000s, surge again in the late 2010s, and then drop sharply after 2018. The decline after 2020 is real in the recorded data, but it should still be read carefully because recorded incidents can change for administrative reasons as well as social ones.

Even with that caution, the chart establishes an important baseline: the dataset has distinct phases rather than one stable average. That is the first sign that crime in this dataset is concentrated rather than evenly distributed over time.

2. The yearly total hides a strong daily rhythm

Once the data is broken into weekday and hour, a much sharper structure appears. Incidents do not arrive uniformly across the 24-hour cycle. Instead, they build into clear peaks, and different categories contribute to those peaks in different ways. Theft-related categories have a strong evening profile, while the overall heatmap shows that some weekday-hour combinations consistently absorb more incidents than others.

This interactive figure is intentionally author-guided in placement but reader-driven in detail. The overall claim is fixed: time matters. But the reader can still inspect which categories dominate specific hours and how the pattern differs across the week.

3. The same concentration appears in space

The temporal structure is matched by a spatial one. When incidents are aggregated into hotspot cells, the map pulls attention toward a relatively small set of central and high-activity areas instead of spreading intensity evenly across San Francisco. That does not mean those neighborhoods are defined by crime, nor does it mean residents there are uniquely responsible for the pattern.

A hotspot map mainly tells us where incidents are repeatedly recorded. In a city with dense commercial corridors, tourism, transit, and heavy policing, that makes these locations especially important for interpretation and especially risky to oversimplify.

What the reader should take away

The main lesson is not that every part of San Francisco became uniformly safer or uniformly riskier over time. The stronger claim is narrower and more defensible: in this dataset, incidents are concentrated. They cluster into specific periods and specific places, which means that any serious interpretation has to be localized instead of relying on citywide averages alone.

That is also why caution matters. Incident data is shaped by enforcement and reporting systems, not just by underlying behavior. The visualizations above are useful because they reveal structure, but they should not be mistaken for a complete causal story.
