Drug Offense in San Francisco is a concentrated record, not a citywide condition

I focus on 2018-2024, where the category label is consistent and the COVID break is visible. Three views tell the same story: Drug Offense is concentrated in Tenderloin, drops sharply during 2020-2021, and follows a daytime rhythm that almost matches Warrant. That makes it look less like a neutral map of drug use and more like a record of where visible drug activity and police contact meet.

Dataset and framing

The dataset is the combined San Francisco Police Department incident file we worked with in Weeks 1-7. Each row is one recorded police incident. That matters here, because a recorded Drug Offense is not the same thing as all drug use in a neighborhood. It depends on public visibility, police presence, and enforcement choices.

I chose this category because Assignment 1 already hinted that it behaves differently from broad property-crime categories. It is unusually concentrated in one district, and it has a very strong relationship with Warrant. That made it a better storytelling candidate than a generic citywide trend.

Question

If Drug Offense is shaped by enforcement as well as underlying activity, what should the data look like? I would expect it to be selective: concentrated in a few places, concentrated at certain hours, and unstable when street conditions change.

1. The first warning sign is how uneven the category already looks

I start with the simplest view: yearly counts and district concentration. The timing matters immediately. Drug Offense falls from 3,404 incidents in 2019 to 2,054 in 2021, then rebounds. Because that break lines up exactly with the COVID years, I do not think it can be read as a clean measure of how much drug use changed underneath. Too many other things changed at the same time: mobility, street life, and policing patterns.

The district panel is even more telling. Tenderloin's Drug Offense share is about five times the citywide baseline, while most districts sit below average. That is not a gentle citywide gradient. It already looks like a selective recording process concentrated in one part of San Francisco.

2. The daily pattern looks more like police contact than nightlife

The second figure adds time-of-day structure. The heatmap peaks from late morning into the afternoon across most weekdays. That is not what I would expect if Drug Offense were mainly a general nightlife category. It looks much closer to a daytime street-enforcement rhythm.

The lower panel makes the point sharper by comparing Drug Offense with Warrant. In my Assignment 1 analysis, this pair had the strongest weekly relationship (R2 = 0.927). Their hourly curves almost overlap here as well. My reading is that these two categories often come out of the same police encounter: someone is stopped, drugs are found, and warrant status is checked at the same time.

3. At street scale, the map collapses into a Tenderloin-centered cluster

The map is the spatial version of the same story. Once I aggregate Drug Offense into rounded street cells, the strongest hotspots do not spread across the city. They cluster around the Tenderloin core and nearby downtown blocks. A small part of San Francisco accounts for a very large share of the recorded incidents.

Outside reporting helps here. City reporting on open-air drug market enforcement repeatedly points to Tenderloin and South of Market as focus areas. That does not prove a single causal story, but it does make the hotspot pattern easier to interpret. The map looks like the overlap of visible street drug activity and sustained police attention.

Takeaway

What makes this category interesting is that every figure points in the same direction. Drug Offense is concentrated in one district, concentrated in a small set of street cells, and concentrated in a daytime schedule that mirrors Warrant.

So my conclusion is not "Tenderloin has five times more drug use." That would claim more than police incident data can support. A narrower conclusion fits the data much better: in this dataset, Drug Offense behaves like a public, enforcement-shaped category. It tells us where drug activity becomes visible enough to be recorded, not where all drug use exists.
